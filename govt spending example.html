Effect of government spending

After-tax income:

$100,000 when young
$200,000 when old

There are no liquidity constraints. Consumers practice consumption smoothing. We know from our earlier work that in this case Ricardian equivalence holds: tax cuts cannot have any effect on GDP.

Question. The government spends money on infrastructure in the amount of $1000 per person when young. It is financed by increasing taxes $1000 per person when old. Calculate the change in consumption when young and old. Then calculate the change in demand when young and old.

Answer. There is no change in income when young or old. There is an additional $1000 in taxes per person when old. That means LATI falls by $1000 per person. Because of consumption smoothing, consumption falls by only $500 per person when young and $500 per person when old.

The net effect on demand is an increase of $500 per person when young (the $1000 of government spending per person minus the $500 in lower consumption). The net effect on demand is a decrease of $500 per person when old (the $500 in lower consumption - there’s no change in government spending when old).

This is demand shifting. Some of the demand (and thus GDP) from old shifted into young because of the government spending. That’s because government spending happens entirely when young, but the reduction in consumption is spread out over many years. Why would you do this? If there’s a recession when young, it’s reasonable to shift some of the future high output when old into demand when young.
